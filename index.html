Human Anatomy Vocabulary

Understanding the correct terminology for human body parts is essential for medical professionals.

Head & Brain

  • Cranium - The skull that protects the brain
  • Cerebrum - The largest part of the brain
  • Cerebellum - Controls coordination and balance
  • Medulla Oblongata - Controls involuntary functions
  • Temporal Lobe - Processes auditory information

Thorax & Cardiovascular

  • Myocardium - Heart muscle tissue
  • Ventricles - Lower chambers of the heart
  • Atria - Upper chambers of the heart
  • Pulmonary Artery - Carries blood to the lungs
  • Aorta - The largest artery in the body

Abdomen & Digestive System

  • Liver - Detoxifies and produces proteins
  • Pancreas - Produces digestive enzymes
  • Duodenum - First part of small intestine
  • Jejunum - Middle section of small intestine
  • Ileum - Final section of small intestine

Limbs & Musculoskeletal

  • Femur - Thigh bone, the longest in the body
  • Humerus - Upper arm bone
  • Phalanges - Finger and toe bones
  • Deltoid - Shoulder muscle
  • Quadriceps - Group of four thigh muscles

Medical Electronic Equipment

Modern healthcare relies on sophisticated electronic equipment for diagnosis and treatment.

Electrocardiogram (ECG/EKG)

A device that records the electrical activity of the heart over a period of time. It detects heart abnormalities by measuring the electrical activity generated by the heart as it contracts.

Primary Use: Cardiac monitoring and diagnosis of heart conditions.

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

A medical imaging technique that uses a magnetic field and computer-generated radio waves to create detailed images of the organs and tissues in the body.

Primary Use: Detailed imaging of soft tissues, organs, and the musculoskeletal system.

Mechanical Ventilator

A machine designed to provide mechanical ventilation by moving breathable air into and out of the lungs to deliver breaths to a patient who is physically unable to breathe or breathing insufficiently.

Primary Use: Respiratory support for patients who cannot breathe adequately on their own.

Ultrasound Machine

A device that uses high-frequency sound waves to create images of structures inside the body. The sound waves are emitted by a probe and the echoes are recorded and displayed as images.

Primary Use: Imaging of soft tissues, blood flow, and fetal development during pregnancy.

Usage Guidelines for Medical Equipment

Proper usage of medical equipment is crucial for accurate diagnosis, effective treatment, and patient safety.

General Safety Protocols

  • All operators must be properly trained before using any medical equipment.
  • Equipment should be inspected before each use to ensure proper functioning.
  • Operators must wear appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE) when required.
  • Emergency shutdown procedures should be clearly understood by all operators.
  • Equipment must not be modified without manufacturer approval.

Electrical Safety

  • All equipment must be properly grounded to prevent electrical hazards.
  • Operators should check for frayed cords or damaged plugs before use.
  • Equipment must not be used near water unless specifically designed for such environments.
  • Circuit overloading should be avoided by distributing equipment across multiple circuits.
  • In case of electrical malfunction, operators must disconnect power immediately.

Patient Safety

  • Patient identity must be verified before any procedure.
  • Equipment should be positioned to avoid patient injury or discomfort.
  • Operators must explain procedures to patients in understandable terms.
  • Patient vital signs should be monitored during procedures when appropriate.
  • Emergency response equipment must be readily available during procedures.

Maintenance Requirements

  • Equipment must be calibrated according to manufacturer specifications.
  • Regular maintenance should be performed and documented.
  • Only qualified technicians should perform repairs or adjustments.
  • Equipment must not be used if maintenance is overdue.
  • Maintenance records should be kept for the lifetime of the equipment.
